The shape of the trip

Four bases, one quiet pace.

Minimal packing-and-moving. We settle into four hotels - one travel-night at Skamania Lodge in the Gorge, then back-to-back doubles at Cannon Beach, Kalaloch in Olympic NP, and Seattle. The route is one-way north with no backtracking, threading the Columbia Gorge, Oregon coast, Olympic Peninsula, and Puget Sound.

Haystack Rock, all day.

A no-driving day - or near enough. We time the morning to low tide for tidepool exploration around Haystack Rock, then walk the beach to lunch, drive five minutes to Hug Point's sea caves and waterfall, and end on the cliffs of Ecola State Park.

Haystack Rock at low tide
Verify the Jul 14 morning low-tide window via NOAA before you head out (mid-July tides shift hour-to-hour from August). At low water, the western reef around Haystack opens up: tidepools full of green anemones, purple ochre stars, hermit crabs, gooseneck barnacles. Mid-July is peak tufted puffin nesting season - bring binoculars. Volunteers from Friends of Haystack Rock are usually on the sand to help you ID things.

Off the typical route

Hidden gems we built into the days.

A handful of side-trips and small-town secrets the guidebooks underplay. None require a major detour - they're tucked into the existing routing - but knowing they're coming makes the days better.

Day 3 · Cannon Beach
Hug Point's hidden waterfall
Most visitors stop at Cannon Beach proper and miss this. At low tide, walk a quarter-mile north around the headland to a sea cave with a 15-foot waterfall pouring directly onto the sand. The original wagon road is still carved into the cliff above. Locals call it.
Best at low tide · 5 mi south of Cannon Beach
Day 5 · Olympic NP
World's Largest Sitka Spruce, Lake Quinault
Forget the Hoh - the Quinault Rainforest has the actual world's-largest specimens. A 1,000-year-old Sitka spruce, 191 feet tall and 17 feet across, is on a signed pull-off path that 99% of NP visitors miss. Three other "world's largest" trees of different species are within 5 miles - the area is officially the Valley of the Rain Forest Giants.
Open year-round · free · 35 mi south of Kalaloch
Day 5 · Olympic NP
Tree of Life at Beach 4
A Sitka spruce hanging suspended over a sea cave with all its roots exposed - it's been clinging to thin air for decades, defying gravity. Most park guides don't mark it, but it's a half-mile south of the Beach 4 trailhead toward Kalaloch. Photographers obsess. Children stare.
Walk south from Beach 4 · 0.5 mi · low to mid-tide
Day 6 · En route
Devil's Punchbowl swimming hole, Lake Crescent
A short detour off our route at Lake Crescent. Park at the Spruce Railroad Trail, walk 0.5 miles east along the old rail bed, and you'll find a turquoise glacier-fed swimming hole below the original Devil's Punchbowl footbridge. The water is 50F and shocking, but on a hot July afternoon it's the photo of the trip. Glacial water is so mineral-rich it filters out impurities.
15 min detour from Marymere Falls · summer only · bring a quick-dry towel
Day 4 · Astoria
Astoria Column + balsa-wood gliders
A 125-foot column on a hilltop above Astoria with a hand-painted sgraffito mural depicting Pacific NW history spiraling up the outside. Climb 164 steps to a 360-degree view of the Columbia, the Pacific, and Saddle Mountain on a clear day. The local tradition: buy a $2 balsa wood glider at the gift shop and launch it from the top.
$5/car parking · 10 minutes off route · open dawn-dusk
Day 6 · Port Angeles
Wilder restaurant, Field Arts & Events Hall
If you arrive in Port Angeles before noon and want to skip the Hurricane Ridge cafe, this is the alternative - a Forbes-acclaimed PNW restaurant inside Port Angeles's brand-new performing arts center on the waterfront, with floor-to-ceiling glass facing the Strait of Juan de Fuca and Vancouver Island. Foraged-and-local menu changes weekly.
Lunch 11:30 - 2 PM · reserve · 25 min from Hurricane Ridge
Day 2 · Portland (skip-the-coast option)
Powell's City of Books
The world's largest independent bookstore - one full city block, four floors, color-coded rooms, used + new on the same shelf. If we'd rather sleep at the coast tonight than push through Multnomah Falls, swap Pittock for an hour at Powell's instead. It's a 10-minute walk from the Portland Japanese Garden too, if you want both.
Open daily 10 AM - 9 PM · free · 5 min from US-26
Day 2 · Portland (alt to Pittock)
Portland Japanese Garden
12-acre Japanese garden in Washington Park, widely rated the most authentic outside Japan - eight garden styles including a Tea Garden, Strolling Pond, and Sand & Stone garden, with a Kengo Kuma cultural village at the entrance. It's a 10-minute drive from Pittock; pick one (Pittock for the view, Japanese Garden for the contemplative walk).
Open 10 AM - 6 PM · $22.95/pp · reserve a time slot
Day 2 · Portland (Forest Park)
The Witch's Castle
A moss-covered stone shell of an old comfort station tucked into Forest Park's Lower Macleay Trail - looks like a forgotten ruin from a fairy tale. Easy 0.7-mile each way from the Lower Macleay trailhead, downhill on the way in. Skip if you're tight on time, but if you've already burned an hour at Pittock and have appetite for another short walk, this is the photo people will ask about.
Free · open dawn-dusk · 0.7 mi each way from Lower Macleay
Day 2 · Columbia Gorge
Wahclella Falls Trail (instead of Multnomah)
A 2-mile out-and-back through Tanner Creek Canyon to a 350-foot horsetail waterfall plunging into a deep mossy bowl. We swap this in for the Multnomah Falls hike - same kind of "wow", a fraction of the crowds, no permit, no parking lottery. The trail follows the creek through old-growth Doug-fir, with side waterfalls and basalt cliffs. Then 9 mi west to Multnomah Lodge for lunch with the icon falls visible from the patio - best of both worlds.
$5/day-use fee · no permit · I-84 Exit 40 (Bonneville Dam)
Day 5/6 · Olympic Peninsula (alt lodging)
Domaine Madeleine B&B, Port Angeles
If we ever do an Olympic-Peninsula-only trip, this is the spot. Five-room bluff-top B&B east of Port Angeles overlooking the Strait of Juan de Fuca and Victoria across the water. Five-acre gardens, hot tub on the bluff, gourmet morning breakfast included. Doesn't fit our routing this trip (we're staying at Quileute and Inn at the Market) but worth bookmarking.
Reference only · Port Angeles bluff · 25 min from Hurricane Ridge

Pacific Northwest in July

What to pack.

Mid-July on the Oregon coast and Olympic Peninsula averages mid-60s F daytime with morning fog and low 50s overnight. Inland Hood River runs 80s, Seattle mid-70s. Layering is the whole game. Days are long - sunsets after 9 PM.

Clothing

  • Lightweight rain shell (waterproof, not just water-resistant - Hoh Rainforest is 12 ft of rain a year)
  • Fleece or wool mid-layer
  • Long pants for hiking + jeans for evenings
  • Shorts for Hood River + dunes
  • Quick-dry trail pants (wet sand + rainforest mud)
  • Two t-shirts + two long-sleeves merino
  • One nice dinner outfit per person (esp. for Seattle)
  • Beach towel + swimsuit (lodges have hot tubs)

Footwear + gear

  • Trail runners or light hikers with grip - mossy rocks at tidepools
  • Sandals or slip-ons for the beach
  • Daypack (15-20L) for hikes
  • Headlamp (for Kalaloch beach fires + cabin)
  • Binoculars (8x42 ideal) - puffins, whales, sea lions, bald eagles in Hoh
  • Polarized sunglasses (cuts glare on wet sand)
  • Reusable water bottles + 2 thermoses

Don't forget

  • Multnomah Falls timed-entry permit (Recreation.gov, 14 days out)
  • Pre-paid Allegiant baggage tags
  • America the Beautiful park pass (covers Olympic NP entry)
  • Washington Discover Pass (or pay $10/day at Cape Disappointment)
  • Sunscreen + lip balm with SPF
  • Anti-nausea meds (windy coast roads, Astoria-Megler bridge)
  • Phone car mount + Spotify offline playlists (no signal anywhere in Olympic NP)
  • Tide chart app (Tide Charts or similar)
  • Mosquito spray for the rainforest day
